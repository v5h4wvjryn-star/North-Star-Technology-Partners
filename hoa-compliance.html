Texas SB 711 — What It Means for Your HOA

Passed by the 89th Texas Legislature and signed into law, effective September 1, 2025.

The Plain-Language Version

Texas has long had some HOA transparency requirements, but SB 711 significantly expanded them. For the first time, the law creates a clear website mandate — not just document-access rights, but a publicly accessible online presence.

Texas Property Code §207.006

"A property owners' association must maintain a website if the association is composed of at least 60 lots, or has contracted with a management company."

The law doesn't just require a website to exist — it requires specific content to be posted and kept current. And the enforcement mechanism is financial: associations that aren't compliant lose significant legal remedies.

A companion bill, SB 2586, adds reporting requirements to TREC (the Texas Real Estate Commission) with daily penalties for non-compliance.

⚠️ Financial Risks of Non-Compliance
1
Loss of attorney fee recovery — If your HOA needs to take legal action against a delinquent homeowner while out of compliance, you bear the full legal cost.
2
Loss of interest on delinquent assessments — You forfeit the right to collect interest during any period of non-compliance.
3
TREC daily penalties — Up to $1,000/day for associations that fail to file rules and documents with TREC as required by SB 2586.
🏘️

60+ Lots or Units

If your subdivision or planned community has 60 or more lots — including any type of residential HOA — you are required to have a compliant website.

🏢

Using a Management Company

Even if your HOA has fewer than 60 lots, if you've contracted with a professional management company, the website requirement applies to you.

🏠

Condominiums

Condo associations with 60+ units are covered under Texas Property Code §82.1142, with the same website requirements and the same compliance deadline.
